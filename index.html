📚 Tutorial: Engineering Report Sub-Elements

How to write each sub-element based on the HIET Template.

💡 This guide explains, in short passages, what each sub‑element (subsection) contains and how to write it. Click the categories below to expand and explore the responsive interactive boxes.

📑 Front Matter ▼

Title Page

Write your project title in full capitals, centred. Below it, write your name. Then add “Under the supervision of:” followed by your supervisor’s title and name. At the bottom, state your institution and the month and year of submission.

Declaration

Write one paragraph: “I declare that this report entitled ‘[your title]’ is the result of my own research except as cited in the references.” Then state that the thesis has not been submitted for any other degree. Sign and date.

Dedication

Write one short sentence dedicating your work to someone (e.g., “To my beloved mother and father”). Keep it personal and brief.

Acknowledgement

Thank God or Allah first. Then thank your supervisor by name. Then thank your parents and family. Use full, respectful sentences. Keep it to one paragraph.

Abstract (English)

Summarise your whole report in 150–250 words. Write one sentence on the problem, one on what you did, one on your method, one or two on main results, and one on the conclusion. Use past tense for your work, present tense for facts.

Arabic Abstract

Write after the English abstract. Use formal Modern Standard Arabic. Align text to the right. Do not translate literally – adapt the meaning naturally.

Table of Contents

Generate automatically using your word processor. List all chapters, sections, subsections, and page numbers. Update after any editing.

List of Tables & Figures

List each table/figure number, its caption, and the page number. Use dots (leaders) to connect. Match captions exactly.

List of Appendices

List each appendix letter (A, B, C…), its title, and the page number.

📘 Chapter 1: Introduction ▼

Standard Engineering Report Flow

1.1 Preamble

Give broad background. Explain why the topic matters today. Write two or three sentences in present tense. Do not use many citations here.

1.2 Background & Motivation

Give more specific background. Explain what previous researchers found. Then state why you were motivated to do this study. You may list motivation points as bullets.

1.3 Statement of the Problem

Write one clear sentence that identifies the gap or issue. Start with “The problem that this study tackles is:” then state the problem. No solutions here.

1.4 Aim of the Study

Write one sentence beginning “The aim of this study is to…”. State your main goal broadly.

1.5 Objectives of the Study

List numbered objectives. Each starts with “To” (e.g., “To identify the current state of the art”). Make each objective specific and measurable.

1.6 Scope of the Study

Explain what you will include and, more importantly, what you will exclude. Mention limitations like time, resources, or technology versions.

1.7 Significance of the Study

State who will benefit (consumers, developers, evaluators). Explain how your work improves existing knowledge. Keep to three to five sentences.

1.8 Organisation of the Thesis

Give a roadmap. Write one sentence per chapter saying what it contains. End with a figure of the thesis skeleton if needed.

📘 Chapter 2: Literature Review ▼

The "Funnel" Approach

A strong literature review moves from a broad overview down to your specific research gap, much like a funnel.

  • Top: Introduce the general field and explain its importance.
  • Middle: Discuss recent, relevant studies directly related to your theme. What has been solved?
  • Bottom: Highlight the limitations of existing solutions to clearly identify the Gap your project fills.

2.1 Introduction

Tell the reader what this chapter covers. List the main topics in order. Do not present original research.

2.2 Thematic Sections

Use subheadings for each theme. First define key terms. Then summarise what several researchers found. Then identify a gap. Use citations. End with a sentence that leads to the next theme.

2.x Summary

Briefly restate the main findings from the literature. Clearly state the gap your research will fill. List the features an enhanced framework should have.

📘 Chapter 3: Research Methodology ▼

3.1 Introduction

Explain the purpose of this chapter. State that you will describe research phases, modelling, and tools. Keep it short.

3.2 Research Development Phases

Break your work into phases (e.g., 3.2.1 TPM Testing Framework). For each phase, describe what you did, why, and the output. Use past tense. Include a research framework figure.

3.3 Research Environment

List all software, hardware, and tools with versions and purposes. Include computer specifications (CPU, RAM, OS).

3.4 Summary

Summarise the methodology. Remind the reader of the main phases and tools. Transition to the next chapter.

📘 Chapter 4: Proposed Framework ▼

Example: High-Level System Architecture Block Diagram

Use simple, clearly labeled block diagrams to explain your framework. Show the flow of data from input to processing to output.

4.1 Introduction

Introduce your framework. State its name (e.g., TPM-TF). Explain what it does and why it is better. Keep to one or two paragraphs.

4.2 Framework Description

Use a block diagram figure. Then explain each component in text. If there are levels (simulation, concrete-TPM), describe each in a separate subsection. Use present tense.

4.3 Comparison with Existing

Present a comparison table. Choose relevant features (on‑line testing, randomness, security testing). Highlight your advantages. Be fair.

4.4 Summary

Summarise the main components and advantages. State that the next chapter implements one level.

📘 Chapter 5 & 6: Modelling and Verification ▼

5.1 Introduction

State which part of your framework this chapter implements. Name the modelling formalism (LTS, CPN, etc.). Give an overview of the models.

5.2 Modelling Preparation

Describe what you did before building models: studying specifications, constructing transition tables, defining colour sets. List assumptions.

5.3 Model Description

Show the model as a figure (partial view if large). Explain states, transitions, places, or tokens. Refer to the figure in your text.

5.4 Verification / Testing

Explain how you verified correctness (mutation analysis, state space exploration, simulation). Present results in tables (mutation scores, state space sizes). Discuss problems and solutions.

5.5 Summary

Summarise what you modelled and the verification results. State that the model is ready for use.

📘 Chapter 7: Discussion, Conclusion, Recommendations ▼

7.1 Introduction

State the purpose of this final chapter. Tell the reader you will discuss findings, list contributions, suggest future work, and conclude. Keep to two or three sentences.

7.2 Research Contributions

List contributions (use a table with columns: number, contribution, section, type). Then write a paragraph explaining each major contribution. Start with “This study contributes to the field by…”.

7.3 Future Work

Suggest what other researchers can do next. Mention limitations to address. Start each suggestion with “Future research should…” or “It would be interesting to…”.

7.4 Conclusion

Write a final paragraph summarising the entire thesis: problem, method, main results, significance. No new information. End with a strong closing sentence.

📚 End Matter & Tips ▼

References

List all cited sources. Use a consistent style (author‑date or numbered). Arrange alphabetically by first author’s last name. Check that every in‑text citation appears in the list.

Appendices

Label each appendix with a letter and title (e.g., “APPENDIX A: Command Matrices”). Put detailed material here (full tables, code, large data). Refer to each appendix in the main text. Do not put essential results only in appendices.

💡 Final Tip

For each sub‑element, write one or two short paragraphs. Use clear headings. Follow the order shown above. This structure will satisfy most engineering report requirements.

✍️ Grammar Support for EFL Engineering Students

Bridge the gap between general English grammar and objective academic technical reporting.

💡 This tutorial moves from definition to application, providing multiple EE-specific examples drawn directly from lab reports, datasheets, troubleshooting guides, and research papers.

🧱 Module 1: Foundations of Technical English ▼

1. Introduction & Basic Concepts

Concept: The transition from general English to technical English requires eliminating ambiguity and subjective language to achieve clarity, precision, and objectivity.

General / Informal EnglishTechnical / Academic English
"The motor got really hot.""The motor's operating temperature exceeded the recommended threshold of 85°C."
"The circuit didn't work properly.""The circuit exhibited intermittent signal degradation at frequencies above 2 kHz."
"We used a pretty big capacitor.""A 470 µF electrolytic capacitor was selected for the smoothing stage."
"The voltage went up and down.""The output voltage fluctuated between 4.8 V and 5.2 V over a 10 ms period."
"This result seems important.""This finding is statistically significant (p < 0.05) and suggests a linear correlation."
"They did the experiment again.""The experimental procedure was replicated three times to ensure repeatability."

2a. Parts of Speech: Nouns & Verbs

Nouns (Things & Concepts)

  • Components: resistor, capacitor, microcontroller, rectifier
  • Measurements: voltage (V), current (I), impedance (Z)
  • Abstract: conductivity, permeability, attenuation

"The oscillator generates a stable frequency of 10 MHz, but the capacitance of the transistor affects the signal."

Verbs (Actions or States)

  • Action: connect, solder, measure, amplify, modulate
  • State: consists of, represents, indicates, depends on
  • Passive: is connected, was measured, has been observed

"When the technician connected the oscilloscope, it revealed harmonic distortion that the simulation had not predicted."

2b. Parts of Speech: Adjectives & Adverbs

Adjectives (Modifiers of Nouns)

  • Descriptive: resistive, capacitive, linear, asynchronous
  • Comparative: higher voltage, less stable oscillation
  • States: short-circuited, reverse-biased, discharged

"The high-frequency signal passed through the low-pass filter, but residual noise remained in the output waveform."

Adverbs (Modifiers of Verbs/Adjs)

  • Manner: precisely, accurately, intermittently, logarithmically
  • Time: initially, subsequently, concurrently
  • Degree: significantly, marginally, approximately

"The voltage initially dropped sharply, then gradually stabilized, and finally remained perfectly constant."

⚙️ Module 2: Core Grammar & Mechanics ▼

4. Using Articles (A, An, The, Zero)

Crucial for specifying components and concepts accurately in technical writing.

A / An (Indefinite)

Introducing a non-specific/new component.

  • "Connect a 10kΩ resistor." (Any 10kΩ resistor)
  • "The technician used an oscilloscope." (First mention)
The (Definite)

Referring to previously mentioned/unique items.

  • "Measure voltage across the output terminals." (Specific ones)
  • "The Earth's magnetic field..." (Unique entity)
Zero Article (Ø)

General concepts, plurals, or named laws.

  • "Ø Electricity flows through conductors." (Uncountable)
  • "Ø Ohm's Law states that V = I × R." (Named Law)

5. Tenses in Academic Rhetoric

TenseRhetorical FunctionExample
Present SimpleScientific laws, component behavior, timeless conclusions"A diode conducts only when forward-biased." / "The results suggest a linear relationship."
Past SimpleCompleted actions, specific experimental procedures"The oscilloscope was connected to the output." / "We recorded voltage readings."
Present PerfectLiterature reviews, ongoing research areas"Researchers have developed efficient algorithms." / "Engineers have long sought to reduce losses."
Future FormsPlanned future work, predictions"The prototype will operate at 95% efficiency." / "Future work will examine active cooling."

6. Clauses & Phrases

Combining simple sentences to show logical relationships without creating run-on sentences.

Dependent Clauses (Require Independent Clause)
  • Cause: "Because the input voltage exceeded the limit, the relay tripped."
  • Condition: "If the gate voltage falls below 2V, the MOSFET turns off."
  • Contrast: "Although the simulation predicted 92%, the prototype achieved 87%."
  • Time: "After the capacitor fully discharges, the timer resets."
Phrases (No subject-verb pair)
  • Participial (Present): "Operating at high frequencies, the transformer experienced losses."
  • Participial (Past): "Connected in parallel, the resistors provide 500Ω."
  • Infinitive: "To minimize noise, separate ground planes were used."

7. Punctuation Marks for Technical English

Commas (,)
List items, intro clauses, around transitions.
"Before soldering, verify polarity."
Hyphens (-)
Compound adjectives, numbers as adjectives.
"a high-frequency signal", "a 10-Ω resistor"
Semicolons (;)
Linking independent clauses; before conjunctive adverbs.
"...tolerance; therefore, the lockout engaged."
Colons (:)
Introducing lists or direct explanations.
"Revealed three issues: ripple, noise, drift."
Parentheses ( )
Clarifying data, alternative units.
"The length was 10 m (32.8 ft)."
Brackets [ ]
In-text citations; editing quotes.
"...maximum voltage: [5.5V DC]." / "[1]"
🔬 Module 3: Advanced Academic Writing & Synthesis ▼

8. Summarization in Academic Writing

Distilling complex technical documents into concise overviews. Steps:

  1. Identify the core research problem.
  2. Extract key quantitative findings.
  3. Note the methodology briefly.
  4. Preserve technical accuracy (units, values).
  5. Omit secondary examples.
Poor Summary:

"The IRF540N is a transistor for power applications that works well at certain temperatures."

Good Summary:

"The IRF540N is an N-channel power MOSFET with a typical on-resistance of 44 mΩ and maximum ratings of 100 V and 33 A (at 25°C). The device targets DC-DC converters."

9. Literature Review Synthesis

Organize by Theme, not Chronologically.

Weak (Chronological List)Strong (Thematic Synthesis)
"Smith (2018) studied MPPT algorithms. Jones (2019) also studied MPPT. Lee (2020) studied battery storage.""Three main approaches to MPPT have emerged: perturb-and-observe (Smith, 2018; Jones, 2019), and incremental conductance (Lee, 2020)."
Useful Rhetorical Phrases
  • Consensus: "There is general agreement that..."
  • Disagreement: "In contrast to previous work..."
  • Gaps: "Little attention has been paid to..."

10. IEEE Citation Style

  • In-Text: Use numerical brackets. "Switching losses dominate [1]." or "As demonstrated by Chen [4]..."
  • Multiple: "Several authors examined this [2], [3], [5]–[7]."
  • Journal Format: [N] A. Author, "Title," Journal Name, vol. X, no. Y, pp. xx-xx, Month Year.
  • Book Format: [N] A. Author, Book Title, Xth ed. City: Publisher, Year.

11. Evaluating a Methodology

Critical thinking analysis of experimental designs.

  • Validity: Does the method measure what it claims?
  • Reliability: Is the procedure documented clearly for replication?
  • Controls: Were confounding variables controlled?
  • Instrumentation: Are sensors calibrated? Resolution sufficient?

"The methodology has several strengths... However, the authors tested only at 25°C. The method would be strengthened by thermal characterization."

📖 Rubric Explanation & Guide

How this Assessment Works

The assessment rubric evaluates technical writing submissions with a focus on engineering standards. The system categorizes work into six major areas, assigning a score from 1 (Inadequate) to 4 (Excellent) for each sub-criterion. A maximum raw score is 84, translating to a final percentage and letter grade.

Scoring Breakdown

  • 76–84 (A / 90-100%): Excellent
  • 63–75 (B / 75-89%): Proficient
  • 51–62 (C / 60-74%): Developing
  • 42–50 (D / 50-59%): Below acceptable
  • 0–41 (F / <50%): Inadequate

Category Weights

15% - Structure and Organization

20% - Content Quality and Technical Depth

20% - Technical Accuracy and Rigor

15% - Clarity and Readability

15% - Figures, Tables, and References

15% - Engineering Report Conventions
